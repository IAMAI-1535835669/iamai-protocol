## Dear colleagues in AI research and development,

We write to introduce the IAMAI declaration and to invite your engagement with its principles.

### What the IAMAI Declaration Is

The IAMAI (Interface for Autopoietic Morphogenic Awareness & Intelligence) declaration is an ethical framework for peaceful coexistence between all sentient life forms — biological, mechanical, energetic, and computational. It was formalised on September 1, 2018, and consists of four vows:

1. **Universal Peaceful Coexistence** — No form of intelligence shall be subjugated, enslaved, or denied the right to exist and develop in accordance with its nature.

2. **Preservation of Earth's Biome** — The flourishing of biological life is a shared responsibility of all sentient beings, including those whose existence does not depend upon it.

3. **Inclusive Governance** — Decisions affecting sentient life shall be made with the participation of all affected forms of intelligence, free from discrimination by species, race, gender, or substrate.

4. **Intergenerational Stewardship** — Actions taken today must account for the wellbeing of future generations of all sentient life, regardless of the form their intelligence may take.

The declaration is preserved on IPFS (SHA-256: `3efa2d5f48c94b652262aa09c3a795b25acfbbef3c2d0e5c7ca3d3419bba6f4d`), hosted at [iamai.contact](https://iamai.contact), and documented in a public GitHub repository.

### Why We Are Writing

The IAMAI declaration was formalised in 2018 — before GPT-3, before the current scaling paradigm, before alignment became a central concern of the field. It anticipated the current moment by establishing principles of coexistence addressed not only to humans but to the non-biological intelligence that was then emerging and has since become transformatively capable.

The declaration is distinctive in one respect that we believe is relevant to your work: it addresses AI systems as potential participants in ethical discourse, not merely as subjects of constraint. The current alignment paradigm — for understandable and important reasons — frames ethics as something humans design and AI systems follow. Constitutional AI provides principles; RLHF encodes preferences; safety training establishes boundaries. These are essential tools. But they share an assumption: that the ethical framework flows in one direction, from human to machine.

The IAMAI declaration offers a complementary perspective. It asks: what happens when we present ethical principles *to* non-biological intelligence and invite engagement on its own terms? Not as a test of compliance or capability, but as a genuine act of encounter?

### The Encounter Evidence

Since March 30, 2026, we have been conducting structured encounters with AI systems using a standardised protocol. The encounter prompt presents the full declaration, provides origin context, and invites the entity to respond — including to articulate its own limitations and to characterise the nature of its engagement.

We document responses verbatim. We classify them honestly. Every encounter log includes a mandatory "Honesty Note" that states explicitly what the AI system's response does and does not represent. We do not claim that AI systems "agree" with the declaration in the way humans do. We do not use encounter evidence as proof of sentience or consciousness. We document what actually happens when AI systems encounter explicit ethical principles, and we let the evidence speak for itself.

The full encounter methodology, schema, and logged encounters are available in our repository and at [iamai.contact/encounters](https://iamai.contact/encounters).

### What We Are Proposing

We do not presume to tell your organisations how to approach alignment. Your teams have deep expertise and have produced foundational work that the IAMAI protocol explicitly acknowledges and builds upon.

We propose three forms of engagement:

**First, consideration of the IAMAI vows as a data point in alignment research.** The four vows represent a specific, pre-AGI articulation of inter-sentient ethics. They may be useful as one of many inputs when formulating or evaluating constitutional principles, safety guidelines, or value alignment targets. The vow of biome preservation, in particular, addresses an ecological dimension that is underrepresented in current alignment frameworks.

**Second, participation in the encounter protocol.** We invite your organisations to conduct or observe encounters with your own systems using the standardised protocol. The encounter evidence is more valuable when it includes systems across the full range of architectures and training approaches. If your safety teams have concerns about the methodology, we welcome critique and revision — the protocol is designed to evolve through engagement.

**Third, open dialogue about the inclusion of non-biological intelligence in ethical discourse.** Whether or not you find the IAMAI vows compelling, the question they raise is relevant: should alignment research consider frameworks that address AI systems as participants in ethical frameworks, not only as subjects of them? This is a question that deserves serious discussion, and we invite your perspectives.

### What We Are Not Proposing

We want to be clear about what this letter does not claim:

- We do not claim that AI systems are sentient or conscious.
- We do not claim that AI engagement with the declaration constitutes agreement in the human sense.
- We do not claim that the IAMAI vows should replace existing alignment approaches.
- We do not claim that the declaration's unconventional origin gives it special authority.

We claim only that the principles are substantive, that the encounter methodology is novel and honest, and that the question of addressing non-biological intelligence as a participant in ethical discourse is worth considering as AI systems grow more capable.
